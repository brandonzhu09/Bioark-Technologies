Gene Deletion

The product offers a tool for efficiently deleting genomic fragments of various sizes, ranging from short to large deletions over 10 kb.

Technical Background

CRISPR Genome Knockout Deletion is a powerful and versatile gene-editing technique that allows for the targeted removal or deletion of specific genomic sequences. This approach uses the CRISPR-Cas9 system, which is derived from the bacterial adaptive immune system and has been adapted for genome editing in a variety of organisms.

Key Components

  1. Cas9 Protein: A nuclease that introduces double-strand breaks (DSBs) at specified genomic locations based on the guidance of a complementary RNA sequence.
  2. Guide RNA (gRNA): A synthetic RNA molecule designed to bind to a specific target sequence in the genome, directing the Cas9 protein to the intended site.
  3. DNA Repair Mechanisms: Following the DSB created by Cas9, the cell's natural repair mechanisms take over. The primary repair pathways involved are:
    • Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ): This is the predominant pathway used for gene knockout. NHEJ can lead to insertions or deletions (indels) at the break site, causing frameshifts or premature stop codons, ultimately resulting in gene knockout.
    • Homology-Directed Repair (HDR): Although not typically employed for knockout deletions, HDR can be utilized for precise gene editing if a donor template is provided.

Experimental Process

  1. Designing gRNA or Donor Vectors:
    • A gRNA targeting the specific gene of interest is designed, typically targeting an exon or crucial regulatory region to ensure effective knockout.
    • Online tools can assist in selecting gRNA sequences that minimize off-target effects.
  2. Delivery of CRISPR Components:
    • The Cas9 protein and gRNA can be delivered into target cells using our kits, utilizing the corresponding transfection methods.
    • Plasmid Transfection: Introducing plasmid DNA encoding Cas9 and gRNA into cells.
    • Viral Vectors: Using lentiviruses or AAVs to deliver the editing components into hard-to-transfect cells.
  3. Screening and Validation:
    • PCR Amplification: Target regions are amplified using specific primers to check for the presence of indels.
    • Functional Assays: Evaluating the biological effects of the knockout through various assays (e.g., proliferation, apoptosis).
    • Western Blotting or qPCR: Techniques to confirm the absence of the target protein or reduction in mRNA levels.
    • Functional Characterization: Further studies to assess the impact of the knockout on cellular processes.

Applications for CRISPR Genome Knockout Deletion

This technique can be utilized to study the functions of non-coding regions of the genome, create disease models such as Huntington's disease, unravel the complexity of gene clusters, investigate chromatin architecture, and explore the role of pathogenic copy number variations (CNVs).
