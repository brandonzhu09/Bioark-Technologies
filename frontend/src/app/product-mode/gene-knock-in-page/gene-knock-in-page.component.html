Gene Knock-In Tagging

The product offers versatile options for attaching selected tags to the 3' and 5' ends of target genes.

Technical Background

Gene knock-in tagging is a technique used to insert specific tags (such as fluorescent proteins, epitope tags, or functional domains) into precise locations in the genome, typically at the 3' or 5' ends of target genes. This allows for real-time tracking of gene expression, localization of proteins, or functional studies of gene products without disrupting the gene's natural regulatory mechanisms. The CRISPR-Cas9 system is the cornerstone of gene knock-in tagging. It employs a guide RNA (gRNA) to direct the Cas9 endonuclease to a specific genomic site, where it creates a double-strand break (DSB). The DSB is then repaired by either non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) or homology-directed repair (HDR). For knock-in tagging, HDR is typically used, where a donor template containing the tag and flanked by homology arms facilitates the precise insertion of the desired tag at the target locus.

Key Components

  1. Cas9 Protein: Creates a DSB at the target site.
  2. Guide RNA (gRNA): Directs Cas9 to the desired genomic locus.
  3. Donor Template: Contains the tag (e.g., GFP, HA tag) along with homology arms to facilitate HDR-mediated knock-in.

Experimental Process

  1. Design of gRNA and donor template vector:
    • The gRNA is designed to target a specific site, usually near the 3' or 5' end of the gene of interest to insert the tag without affecting the gene's function. The gRNA must be optimized to ensure high specificity and minimal off-target effects.
    • The donor template can be a plasmid or single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) and contains the tag flanked by homology arms (~500-1000 bp) that are homologous to the sequences surrounding the Cas9-induced DSB.
    • The tag can be fluorescent markers (e.g., GFP, mCherry), epitope tags (e.g., FLAG, HA), or functional domains (e.g., kinase domains, degradation signals).
  2. Cell Transfection, Screening and validation:
    • The Cas9/gRNA complex and the donor template are delivered into the cells via transfection (e.g., lipofection, electroporation, or viral delivery).
    • In hard-to-transfect cells, alternative delivery methods such as viral vectors (AAV or lentivirus) or RNP (ribonucleoprotein) complexes can be used to increase efficiency.
  3. Screening and Validation:
    • PCR-based screening is performed to confirm the successful integration of the tag into the target gene. Primers are designed to bind outside the homology arms and amplify across the knock-in site.
    • Sequencing is often performed to ensure correct and precise insertion without mutations.
    • If fluorescent tags are used, fluorescence microscopy or flow cytometry can be employed for preliminary screening.
    • After confirmation of the knock-in, Western blotting, immunofluorescence, or co-immunoprecipitation (Co-IP) may be used to validate the presence and functionality of the inserted tag.
