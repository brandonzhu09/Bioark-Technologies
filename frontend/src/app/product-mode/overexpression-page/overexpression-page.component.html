Technical Background

Using CRISPR-Cas9 for targeted knock-in at safe harbor sites involves:

  • Cas9: An endonuclease that creates a double-strand break (DSB) at the target site.
  • Guide RNA (gRNA): Directs Cas9 to the specific safe harbor locus.
  • Donor DNA template: A construct containing the desired transgene flanked by homology arms that are complementary to the sequences adjacent to the DSB, which facilitates targeted insertion via homology-directed repair (HDR).

Safe harbor sites are specific regions in the genome where foreign DNA can be inserted without disrupting vital endogenous gene functions or causing harmful effects to the cell. These sites are often utilized for stable gene insertion in genome engineering because they support long-term and predictable expression of transgenes.

Several common safe harbor loci include:

  • AAVS1 (PPP1R12C locus) on chromosome 19
  • ROSA26 locus in mice
  • H11 locus in human and murine genomes

Experiments Involved in CRISPR Targeted Knock-In at Safe Harbor Sites

Regular Plasmid

  • gRNA Selection: The guide RNA is designed to specifically target the chosen safe harbor site. The sequence must be carefully chosen to maximize on-target specificity and minimize off-target activity.
  • Cas9 Delivery: The CRISPR-Cas9 system can be delivered into cells using various methods such as plasmids, mRNA, or RNP (ribonucleoprotein complexes). Delivery methods depend on the cell type and the specific requirements of the experiment.
  • The donor DNA contains the gene of interest (GOI) along with homology arms (typically 500-1000 bp) that match the sequence flanking the target safe harbor site.
  • Depending on the application, the donor template may include selection markers (e.g., antibiotic resistance genes or fluorescent reporters) to aid in the identification of successfully modified cells.

Stable Cell Line Construction

  • Cell Type: Common cell types used include HEK293, iPSCs, and other therapeutic or industrially relevant cell lines.
  • Transfection: Cells are co-transfected with the gRNA, Cas9, and donor template using transfection methods such as electroporation, lipofection, or viral vectors (e.g., lentivirus or AAV). These methods vary depending on the cell type and efficiency requirements.
  • Antibiotic Selection/Fluorescence Screening: If the donor contains a selection marker, antibiotic resistance or fluorescence-based sorting (e.g., FACS) can be used to enrich cells that have undergone successful knock-in.
  • PCR and Sequencing: PCR is used to amplify the regions flanking the insertion site, followed by sequencing to confirm that the gene of interest was correctly integrated into the safe harbor locus.
  • Validation of Transgene Expression and Function
